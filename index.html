The Myth of the Lone Wolf

The copycat effect in far-right terror

On May 14, 2022 Payton Gendron, clad in military fatigues, opened fire at shoppers inside a Tops Friendly supermarket, in Buffalo, New York.

He was wearing body armour, and a camera connected to the livestream site Twitch. 10 people, all African-American, were killed, and 3 were wounded.

He was 18 years old.

Over the last few decades, mass-casualty events such as the Buffalo shooting have taken place at increasing rates across the globe. Like Gendron, their perpetrators are often young, and male — and in almost every case, they act alone.

These are individuals who — while sharing similar far-right ideologies — have never met or talked to each other, and who most often have no defined political agenda or affiliations. They are ‘lone-actors’, isolated by definition.

But upon a closer look, patterns among them start to emerge.

First, a little bit of context. In the United States of America, only 6% of convicted terrorists are lone actors.

However, that 6% is responsible for a quarter of all terrorism convictions between the 1980s and 2015. To date, they represent the majority of terrorist attacks in the US.

In Europe, too, right-wing lone-actor terrorist killed more than Islamic or ISIS-inspired terror plots from 2000 to 2014.

And across the Western Hemisphere, lone-actors carried out 93% of fatal terrorist attacks over the last five years.

Now — when (and how) did this all start?

Let’s go back to 1978. At the same time as Ted Kaczynski was starting his mail bombing campaign, a novel named ‘The Turner Diaries’ was published by a white supremacist, depicting a violent takeover of the US government.

This novel would later become widely influential and go on to fuel a number of right-wing acts of terror, including the Oklahoma City Bombing in 1995.

It wasn’t until the 2010s, however, when the current standard of copycat terrorism started to emerge, starting with the mass shooting at Oslo and Utoya committed by Anders Breivik. Like Ted Kaczynski, he published a manifesto, and like the Oklahoma bombers, he was also inspired by the Turner Diaries. Five different perpetrators of right-wing violence list him as their source of inspiration — chief among them Brenton Tarrant, the 2019 New Zealand mosque shooter, who live-streamed his attack.

By the early 2010s, social media channels such as 4chan, 8chan, and Twitter (and later, Telegram), had become hubs for amplifying attacks, encouraging would-be perpetrators, and portraying those that engage in violent acts as ‘saints’. It’s through these channels that the idea of these perpetrators as lone-actors falls short of accurate: in their manifestos, there are traces of each other’s writing. They follow each other on social media. They belong to the same ‘Terrorgram Collective’ group chats, they talk to each other. Encourage each other. And copy each other.

Which brings us back to Payton Gendron.

Before his mass shooting, he published a 180 page manifesto, a third of it plagiarising other manifestos, particularly that of Brenton Tarrant. He rambled about the Great Replacement conspiracy, also espoused in the Turner Diaries. And he praised the works of three other prior mass shooters. Like them, he wore body armour, a Go-Pro camera, and an AR-15. He wouldn’t be the last one.

Beyond the Myth

There is a clear trend towards an increased number of attacks, by a younger, but well prepared lone-actor (with some improvements in the last two years). However, the attackers themselves are getting radicalised at a faster rate. Continuing to treat these as one-off events instead of an organised, massive global network, and failing to appropriately monitor platforms such as Telegram, will not be effective in preventing these attacks.
