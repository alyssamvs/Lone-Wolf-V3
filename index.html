The Myth of the Lone Wolf

The copycat effect in far-right terror

On May 14, 2022 Payton Gendron, clad in military fatigues, opened fire at shoppers inside a Tops Friendly supermarket, in Buffalo, New York.
He was wearing body armour, and a camera connected to the livestream site Twitch. 10 people, all African-American, were killed, and 3 were wounded.
He was 18 years old.
A wolf pack operates like a family unit, with clear leadership and organized hunting strategies that require cooperation.
Communication between pack members is sophisticated, involving body language, scent marking, and a range of vocalizations.
Even when wolves do leave their packs, they're typically seeking to start new families, not to live in isolation.
The social networks of wolf packs reveal complex relationships and hierarchies that have evolved over millennia.

Beyond the Myth

The "lone wolf" narrative persists in our culture despite being largely a misconception. Understanding the true social nature of wolves helps us appreciate their remarkable adaptations and the importance of community for survival in challenging environments.

As we continue to study these fascinating creatures, we discover more about their complex social dynamics and the critical role they play in maintaining ecological balance.
